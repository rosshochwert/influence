    It's Hard to be a Tapper
    "The problem is that tappers have been given knowledge (the song title) that makes it impossible for them to imagine what it's like to lack that knowledge. When they're tapping, they can't imagine what it's like for the listeners to hear isolated taps rather than a song. This is the Curse of Knowledge."

    "Once we know something, we find it hard to imagine what it was like not to know it. Our knowledge has "cursed" us. And it becomes difficult for us to share our knowledge with others, because we can't readily re-create our listeners' state of mind"

    Chip and Dan Heath, Made to Stick

    Power
    Definition:
    I will cite the classic French + Raven model of power:
    • Reward
    • Coercive
    • Expert
    • Referent
    • Legitimate
    Examples:
    • Robert Moses knew the rules (expert / legitimate power), got the media to like him (referent power)
    • LBJ created power in little congress by making debates a preview of real topics
    • de Mello acted "below his authority" to increase referent power
    Application at Meta:
    When I first join, there is a 4-6 week recruiting process wherein I interview with different teams. Here is where I hold the most power (initially), as many teams are trying to recruit me. I have some expert power from my non-traditional background (MBA and Data Science)

    As I continue on my journey, I hope to cultivate more expert power by specializing in a certain domain, but focus on referent power (which feeds into ethos as well later on).
    Agency
    Definition:
    Perhaps my favorite concept in the course overall (even if "agentic" feels like a made up word)

    definition: to act, to exert control over one's environment

    On a personal level, the second part of the definition really resonates with me, as I often feel boxed in. While we can't control everything, it's nice to find the elements we can, or be creative (as instructed in this class) and find alternate ways to exert control

    Examples:
    Almost every part of this class has agency...

    I'll just share two quick examples / applications I enjoyed:
    • LBJ creating power for himself through Little Congress
    • de Mello sending payments to victims' families unless stopped by UN (ask for forgiveness, not permission)
    • Related to this is Situational Awareness: in order to control one's environment, one must know the environment they are in!
    Application at Meta:
    Some goals for myself when it comes to agency:
    • Proactively reach out to different teams and interview with them (during team selection process)
    • Make clear my goals for the team (what am I here to learn, what do I prioritize)
    • Knowing that decision-making is decentralized at Meta, take advantage of that. Be aggressive
    • Perform well, and make sure people know it! Meta is a public place: all work is publically available. Get people to like / comment on my research. Make some noise
    Ethics
    Definition:
    Four things to keep in mind:
    • We show implicit prejudice
    • We show in-group favoritism
    • We overclaim credit
    • We resolve conflicts in our favor

    Research shows that people think performance is changeable, but character is static

    Examples:
    • "It takes 20 years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it. If you think about that, you'll do things differently. - Warren Buffet
    • "It's easier to hold your principles 100 percent of the time than it is to hold them 98 percent of the time." - Clayton Christenson
    Application at Meta:
    I want to apply Nakahara's checklist, specifically the "sunshine test", or "front page of the WSJ." At Meta, our research ends up on the front page of the news all the time, so it's very real for me to consider.

    On a more long-term note, I identify as a data person and want to make a career out of it. However, I know that data can be manipulated in an unethical way. I want to commit myself to using data ethically, focusing on rule 3 from Nakahara: "What would I do if I had to make this decision 10 more times? Or 100!"
    Networking
    Concepts (rather than definitions):

    • Structural holes: you want people you know to know you're in the know (not know each other)
    • Efficient ties link you to more people with less effort
    • Balance weak ties with strong ties, weak ties bridge, while strong ties bond. Both important!
    • Reciprocity: help gift resource that is 1.) costly, 2.) unselfish, 3.) unexpected, 4.) difficult to repay in kind
    Applications / Examples:
    A few quick examples I enjoyed:
    • From networking class - make the speech. When you volunteer to make the speech, people will come to you to network
    • From Heidi - host the dinner. When you host the dinner, make sure everyone knows half the people there.
    • Sit near the kitchen!
    • Reach out before you need the favor
    • Little "thinking of you" notes, with the last line being "no need to respond!"
    • "In order to be irreplaceable, one must always be different." - Coco Chanel

    Persuasion
    Definition:
    "A person has two reasons for doing something: a good reason, and the real reason"
    Humans have two systems for thinking:
    1. System 1: Intuitive (automatic, emotional)
    2. System 2: Deliberative (calculated, rational)

    To be persuasive, need to use logos, pathos and ethos

    Examples:
    Logos:
    • Use the data they traffic in
    • Less is more: "Before you leave the house, look in the mirror and take one thing off" - Coco Chanel
    Pathos:
    • Use a diorama! "It never ceases to amaze me how you can talk and talk and talk to some guy about something you’ve got in mind, and he isn’t very impressed, and then you bring in a beautiful picture of it, or better yet, a scale model with the bridge all in white and the water nice and blue, see, and you can see his eyes light up."" - Jack Madigan, assistant to Robert Moses
    • Pictures, examples, music, pose a question, emotion, stories
    Ethos:
    There is a concept called the self-promotion dilemma. You want to state your credentials, but don't want to come off as conceited. One solution is to find a hype-person, another is to watch the self-deprecation.
    Application at Meta:
    Logos:
    People at meta are smart and know data, so I need to think carefully. I made mistakes over the summer with not investing enough into presentations and tailoring:
    • For data scientists: get into the weed, show the p-values, show the assumptions
    • For product managers: use less than needed, focus more on the product recommendation. Assume they trust the numbers, then what should they do?
    Pathos:
    • Use screenshots of the features I'm working on
    • Show the storyboard of an individual user through the app
    Ethos:
    • Have my summer intern manager hype me up!
    • Watch the MBA self-deprecation humor; while I am new to the field, I have a degree in data science that I studied hard for. I am here to learn, but have much to offer!
